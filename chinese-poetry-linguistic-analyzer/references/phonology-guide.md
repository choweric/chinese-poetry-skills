# Phonological Analysis Guide

## Core Principle

Fuller's foundational argument: "Poetry is what is lost in translation." Sound effects—rhyme, tone, cadence—create meanings that have "no easy correspondences when moving from one language to another."

## Three Historical Sound Systems

Understanding Classical Chinese poetry requires knowing **which phonological system** applied when the poem was written. The same characters sounded different across periods.

### Old Chinese (Until early Han, ~206 BC)

**Characteristics:**
- More complex initial consonant clusters than later forms
- Final consonants: -ʔ (glottal stop), -s, -p, -t, -k
- **NO pitch-contour tones** (came later)
- Final consonants evolved into Middle Chinese tones

**Evidence from Phonetic Compounds:**

| Modern Mandarin | Old Chinese Reconstruction | Components |
|---|---|---|
| jīng 京 | *krang | Complex initial cluster |
| liáng 涼 | *C.rang | Prefix + rhyme |
| shǔ 數 | *s-roʔ | Fricative prefix + medial + final stop |
| lǚ 屢 | *roʔ-s | Rhyme + suffix |

**Lost Meanings:**
The prefixes and suffixes were grammatical morphemes whose meanings were "likely lost by the Han dynasty," meaning even traditional Chinese readers "no longer have access to important shades of meaning in those early texts."

**Implication for Poetry:**
Old Chinese poems rhyme on final consonants, NOT tones. Reconstructing pronunciation is essential to understanding rhyme patterns in pre-Han poetry.

---

### Middle Chinese (Han through Tang, 206 BC–907 AD)

**Characteristics:**
- **Four-tone system** replaced Old Chinese finals
- This is the system Classical Tang poetry uses

**The Four Tones:**

| Tone Name | Chinese | Mandarin | Origin |
|---|---|---|
| Level | 平 píng | 1st & 2nd tones | Old Chinese vowel finals |
| Rising | 上 shǎng | 3rd tone | Old Chinese -ʔ glottal stop |
| Departing | 去 qù | 4th tone | Old Chinese -s fricative |
| Entering | 入 rù | (lost in Mandarin) | Old Chinese stops -p, -t, -k |

**Tone-Final Correspondence Example:**

The rhyme group containing 安 (an):
- Level tone: 安 (an)
- Rising tone: 晏 (an)
- Departing tone: 晏 (a:n)
- Entering tone: 揠 (a:t) ← Note the -t final

**Why This Matters:**
"The relationship between tone and final consonant... clarifies why rhyme books [are] organized by the four tones... The entering tone... with its final stops -p, -t, or -k... remained distinct from the first three tones."

---

### Early Mandarin (Late Tang onward, complete by Yuan 1280–1368)

**Key Changes:**
- Entering tone **lost in Mandarin** (but preserved in Cantonese, Taiwanese)
- Middle Chinese tones redistributed into modern Mandarin's four tones
- Some features began appearing "in the late Tang"

**Practical Impact:**
Modern Mandarin pronunciation is **not reliable** for analyzing Tang or earlier poetry. Must use Middle Chinese reconstructions.

## Rhyme in Classical Poetry

### Rhyme Books (韻書)

**Purpose:**
Rhyming dictionaries organized by tone and rhyme group. Primary evidence for Middle Chinese phonology.

**Organization:**
1. Four large sections (one per tone: level, rising, departing, entering)
2. Within each tone: chapters for every possible rhyme
3. **Tongyong** (通用) groups: Near-rhymes acceptable when formal constraints were less strict

**Tang Dynasty Standard:**
The *Qieyun* (切韻, 601 AD) and its expansion *Guangyun* (廣韻, 1008 AD) were authoritative references.

### Rhyme Patterns in Forms

**Regulated Verse (律詩):**
- Rhymes on even-numbered lines (2, 4, 6, 8)
- Optional rhyme on line 1
- All rhymes must be from **same rhyme group**
- Usually **level tone rhymes only**

**Old Style Verse (古體詩):**
- More flexible
- Can change rhyme groups mid-poem
- Can mix tones (though same-tone rhyming preferred)

**Example - Du Fu's "Spring Vista" (春望):**
```
國破山河在 [tsən rhyme, falling tone]
城春草木深 [sim rhyme, level tone]
感時花濺淚 [rhyme-less]
恨別鳥驚心 [sim rhyme, level tone]
```

Lines 2 and 4 rhyme perfectly (both -im finals, level tone).

## Tonal Prosody and Regulated Verse

### Evolution

**Shen Yue (441–513)**: First documented awareness of tonal patterns as prosodic resource

**By Tang Dynasty**: Formalized into "regulated verse" (律詩 lǜshī) conventions with strict tonal alternation rules

### Five-Character Regulated Verse Patterns

Two basic patterns that alternate line by line:

**Pattern A:**
```
仄仄平平仄
平平仄仄平 (rhyme)
```

**Pattern B:**
```
平平平仄仄
仄仄仄平平 (rhyme)
```

Key rules:
- **Mirror-image alternation** within each couplet
- Characters 2, 4, 6 must follow pattern strictly
- Characters 1, 3, 5, 7 more flexible (but patterns preferred)
- 平 (píng) = level tone; 仄 (zè) = deflected tones (rising, departing, entering)

**Example:**
```
國 破 山 河 在
仄 仄 平 平 仄

城 春 草 木 深
平 平 仄 仄 平 (rhyme)
```

### Seven-Character Patterns

Same principles, with two extra characters at beginning:

```
仄仄 + 平平平仄仄
平平 + 仄仄仄平平 (rhyme)
```

### Violation as Technique

**Critical insight from Fuller:**
"The conventions for tonal prosody in Tang regulated verse provided a set of norms **within which, and at times against which**, poets composed their verse."

Poets systematically **violated** tonal patterns for rhetorical effects. Du Fu was famous for this; Huang Tingjian studied Du Fu's violations as models.

**When analyzing:**
- Map the actual tones against the expected pattern
- Ask: Why this violation? What effect does it create?
- Violations often emphasize key words or disrupt expectations for thematic reasons

## Sound Effects Untranslatable in English

### Problem of Poetic Sound

Fuller's key examples of what translation loses:

#### Du Fu's "Spring Vista" - Sonic Unity

The Chinese rhyme scheme creates cohesion English cannot replicate:
```
國破山河在 [tsən falling]
城春草木深 [sim level] ← rhymes
感時花濺淚 [no rhyme]
恨別鳥驚心 [sim level] ← rhymes
```

The -im rhyme creates sonic unity between "deep vegetation" (深) and "startled heart" (心), connecting the natural and emotional realms sonically in a way the English translation "deep" / "heart" cannot.

#### Alliteration and Assonance

Classical Chinese poets used:
- **Repetition of initials** (consonant alliteration)
- **Repetition of finals** (vowel assonance within lines, not just rhymes)

These create aural textures that:
- Unite imagery
- Create emphasis
- Establish rhythm

**English translation loses these entirely.**

## Practical Analysis Steps

### Step 1: Identify Historical Period

Determine which sound system applies:
- Pre-Han → Old Chinese (final consonant rhymes, no tones)
- Han–Tang → Middle Chinese (four-tone system)
- Song onward → Transitioning to Mandarin

### Step 2: Map Rhyme Scheme

For regulated verse:
- Identify rhyme words (lines 2, 4, 6, 8; optional line 1)
- Check if all from same rhyme group
- Note tone category (usually level)

For old style:
- Track where rhymes change
- Note any tone mixing

### Step 3: Analyze Tonal Patterns (for Regulated Verse)

Map actual tones against expected pattern:
```
Expected: 仄仄平平仄
Actual:   仄平平仄仄

Violation at position 2: 仄 → 平
```

Ask: Why this violation? What word is emphasized?

### Step 4: Identify Sound Effects

Look for:
- Repeated initials (alliteration)
- Repeated finals within lines (assonance)
- Onomatopoeia (sound-meaning correspondence)

### Step 5: Explain What Translation Loses

Be explicit about:
- Which rhymes are lost
- What tonal patterns create (that English stress cannot replicate)
- How sound unites imagery or meaning
- Why this matters for interpretation

## Common Pitfalls

### 1. Using Modern Mandarin Pronunciation

**Error:** Analyzing Tang poetry rhyme using modern pronunciation
**Problem:** Entering tones are lost; tone redistribution makes rhyme patterns unrecognizable
**Solution:** Use Middle Chinese reconstructions (available in scholarly dictionaries)

### 2. Assuming Tonal Patterns Are Always Followed

**Error:** Treating violations as "mistakes" or "not real regulated verse"
**Problem:** Misses intentional rhetorical effects
**Solution:** Map violations and ask why the poet chose to break the pattern

### 3. Ignoring Sound Because Translation Is Necessary

**Error:** Accepting that "sound is lost" and not analyzing it
**Problem:** Misses how sound creates meaning
**Solution:** Analyze sound in Chinese, then explicitly state what translation loses and why

### 4. Over-Relying on Rhyme Books Without Historical Context

**Error:** Assuming all rhyme groups were equally acceptable across all periods
**Problem:** Rhyme acceptability changed over time; Tang vs Song standards differed
**Solution:** Consult period-specific scholarship on rhyme conventions

## Key Quotations from Fuller

> "Poetry is what is lost in translation."

> "The linguistic effects of a poem—ranging from the sound, to the cadence, to the ordering of words and structuring of sentences—find no easy correspondences when moving from one language to another."

> "The very title of the poem, 'Spring Vista,' raises the question of what the seasonal cycle of return means for Du Fu and the Tang state, which the poem explores through its juxtapositions of the human and the natural."

> "To understand Chinese poetry, then, we need to begin with a basic understanding of the classical Chinese language in which the works were composed."
