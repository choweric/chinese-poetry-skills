# Syntactic Analysis Guide

## Core Principle

Fuller identifies **four fundamental syntactic relationships** that structure Classical Chinese sentences. Understanding these is essential because Classical Chinese syntax differs fundamentally from English, and these differences create poetic possibilities unavailable in translation.

---

## Overview: The Four Core Relationships

1. **Topic—Comment** (sentence-level structure)
2. **Verb—Object** (three types: direct, indirect, locative)
3. **Modifier—Modified** (flexible word order)
4. **Coordination** (nouns, verbs, phrases, sentences)

---

## 1. TOPIC—COMMENT STRUCTURE

### Fundamental Difference from English

**English**: Subject-prominent language
- Sentences require explicit subjects
- Subject performs action of verb

**Classical Chinese**: Topic-prominent language
- Sentences do NOT require explicit subjects
- Topic = what the sentence is about
- Comment = what the sentence says about the topic

### Basic Structure

```
[TOPIC] [COMMENT]
```

The comment can be:
- A complete sentence (verbal predicate)
- A noun phrase (nominal predicate)

### Example 1: Verbal Comment (Li Bai)

```
長安不見使人愁
Chang'an not see cause person grieve
= "Chang'an cannot be seen, causing one to grieve"
```

**Analysis:**

**First level:**
- Topic: 長安 (Chang'an)
- Comment: 不見 ("[I] do not see [it]") — complete sentence with implicit subject

**Second level (larger structure):**
- Topic: The entire four-character sentence above
- Comment: 使人愁 ("[it] causes one to grieve")

**Fuller's key insight:**
> "Moving 'Chang'an' to the topic position creates a form of rhetorical focus on the phrase... The entire four-character sentence in turn is the topic about which the comment is '[it] causes one to grieve.'"

**Why this matters for poetry:**
Topic fronting allows the poet to:
- Create rhetorical emphasis WITHOUT passive voice (which requires morphological marking in English)
- Move beyond first-person subject ("I") to more universal "one" that allows reader identification
- Create layered sentence structures where whole clauses become topics

### Example 2: Nominal Comment (Identity Statement)

```
夫婿輕薄兒
husband light-slight lad
= "My husband [was] a man of shallow feelings"
```

**Analysis:**

- Topic: 夫婿 (husband)
- Comment: 輕薄兒 (fickle lad) — NOUN PHRASE, not verb

The two verbs 輕 (light) and 薄 (slight) modify the noun 兒 (lad), creating a nominal comment.

**Fuller:**
> "The basic function of a nominal sentence (when the comment is a noun) is to **make identity statements**."

**English translation problem:**
English requires:
- Copula verb ("was")
- Past tense marker

Chinese has neither. The relationship between topic and comment is simply juxtaposition.

### Practical Implications

1. **Pronouns are mostly dropped** in poetry (because topics don't require explicit subjects)
2. **Passive constructions** achieved through topic-fronting, not morphological marking
3. **Emphasis** created by choice of what to place in topic position
4. **Layered structures** where entire sentences become topics of larger sentences

## 2. VERB—OBJECT RELATIONSHIPS

Fuller identifies **three distinct object types**, each with different semantic and syntactic properties.

### 2.1 Direct Objects

**Definition**: The entity acted upon by the verb

**Standard word order:** Verb + Direct Object

```
美人捲珠簾
lovely-one roll bead curtain [d.o.]
= "The beautiful one rolls up the bead curtain"
```

**Analysis:**
- Verb: 捲 (roll)
- Direct Object: 珠簾 (bead curtain)

**English parallel:**
Direct objects work similarly in both languages. No major poetic complications.

---

### 2.2 Indirect Objects

**Definition**: The recipient or beneficiary of an action

**Standard word order:** Verb + Indirect Object + Direct Object

```
與之一杯酒
give him [i.o.] one glass wine [d.o.]
= "[I] gave him one glass of ale"
```

**Fuller:**
> "There of course are some differences between the Classical Chinese and English versions of indirect objects, but they have no significant impact on poetic usage."

---

### 2.3 Locative Objects (CRUCIAL FOR POETRY)

**Definition**: A noun slot marking **location in time, space, or a relational system** that participates in the action

**This is the MOST IMPORTANT object type for poetry** because:
1. It creates unique poetic constructions unavailable in English
2. Its marker (於) is often omitted in poetry, creating intentional ambiguity
3. It enables causative constructions that "animate" landscapes

#### Type 1: Motion Verbs (Most Common)

**Standard structure:** Verb + Locative Object

```
行到水窮處
walk arrive water end place [loc.]
= "Walking, I arrive where the water ends"
```

**Fuller:**
> "Verbs of motion (come, go, enter, leave, etc.) take locations as their objects."

**No function word needed** with motion verbs—the locative object follows directly.

#### Type 2: Comparative Constructions

**Marker:** 於 (yú) introduces the standard of comparison

```
霜葉紅於二月花
frost leaves red [loc.] second-month flower
= "The frosted leaves are redder than the flowers of the Second Month"
```

**Analysis:**
- Verb: 紅 (to be red)
- Locative marker: 於
- Locative object: 二月花 (second-month flowers)

The function word 於 marks the "location in relational space" (the comparison standard).

#### Type 3: Passive Voice Marking

**Marker:** 於 introduces the agent in passive constructions

```
有備則制人
If [one] has preparation, [one] controls people

無備則制於人
If [one] lacks preparation, [one] is controlled [loc.] by people
```

**Fuller's key observation:**
> "It becomes clear that the function word 於 marking the locative object can be **indispensable in clarifying who did what to whom**."

**BUT:**

> "However, 於 is a 'prosy' character usually avoided in poetry so that on occasion **uncertainty can creep into a line**. This syntactic ambiguity becomes a resource that authors can exploit."

#### CRUCIAL POETIC EFFECT: Causative Constructions

**Definition**: Making a stative verb (descriptive) into a causative verb (action) by giving it a direct object

**Without causative reading:**
```
日色冷 = The sun's appearance is cold (stative)
```

**With causative reading (taking direct object):**
```
日色冷青松
sun color cold green pine [d.o.]
= "The sun's appearance **chills** the green pines"
```

**Fuller's analysis:**
> "One can interpret the 'green pines' as the locative rather than direct object of 'cold': 'The sun's appearance is cold **on** the green pines.' Weakening the 冷 from the active verb 'chills' to the stative verb 'cold,' however, **destroys the animated quality** of the landscape Wang Wei describes here."

**Why this matters:**
Causative constructions **syntactically enforce a connection** between elements of a scene that English translation weakens.

**Another example:**
```
墟里上孤煙
hamlet go-up lone smoke [d.o.]
= "The hamlet **sends forth** a lone [column of] smoke"
```

**Alternative reading:**
"Goes up, the lone smoke" (i.e., smoke rises from the hamlet)

**Fuller's argument:**
> "Although one can argue that this word order is a form of 'poetic' inversion... little in the poetic tradition justifies or particularly requires this reading."

The hamlet is the AGENT that sends up the smoke—an animated landscape.

#### Huang Tingjian Example: Intentional Ambiguity

When the 於 marker is omitted (as in poetry), directionality becomes ambiguous:

```
瓦溝鳴急雪
tile channel sing urgent snow

睡鴨照華燈
sleep duck shine florid lamp
```

**Fuller:**
> "This couplet, by the Song dynasty poet Huang Tingjian, is difficult to read with certainty. It is possible that **the roof tiles themselves cause** the wind-driven snow to sing out, or **the tiles are made to sing by** the snow. Similarly, the 'sleeping duck' is a metal incense burner, but are we to see **it in the light of** the lamp, or are we to see **the lamp reflected in** the metal of the incense burner?"

**The ambiguity is intentional** and creates a dynamic interplay between elements of the scene.

## 3. MODIFIER—MODIFIED RELATIONSHIPS

### Core Rule

**Modifier precedes modified** (unlike Romance languages)

This applies to:
- Adjectives modifying nouns
- Adverbs modifying verbs
- Nouns modifying nouns
- Verbs modifying nouns
- Nouns modifying verbs (which become nominalized)

### Flexibility and Ambiguity

Because word classes are fluid, the **same two-character sequence** can have multiple readings:

```
靜山
```

**Reading 1**: Verb modifying noun
- 靜 (to be still) modifies 山 (mountain)
- = "a quiet mountain"

**Reading 2**: Complete sentence
- 山 (mountain) = topic
- 靜 (still) = verbal predicate
- = "The mountain is still"

### Disambiguation with 之

The particle 之 explicitly marks noun-modifying-verb:

```
山之靜
mountain [particle] still
= "the stillness of the mountain"
```

Now 靜 is clearly nominalized ("stillness"), and 山 modifies it.

**Fuller:**
> "The optional character 之, however, serves to **remove the ambiguity** and explicitly inform us that 'mountain' here modifies 'still[ness].'"

### Restricted Modifiers (Review)

**Noun modifiers only:**
- 其 (his, her, its, their)
- 諸 (all the...)

**Verb modifiers only:**
- 不 (not)
- 未 (not yet)
- 所 (function word creating nominal from verb)

**Fuller:**
> "Despite the general ease with which words shift categories in Classical Chinese, there are a few important words that are **specifically restricted** to serving as modifiers of either nouns or verbs."

### Poetic Exploitation: Du Fu's "Bamboo Coolness"

```
竹涼
bamboo cool/coolness
```

- 竹 modifies 涼
- Creates compound meaning unavailable in English: "bamboo-associated coolness"

**Fuller:**
> "The effect of the modification relationships... is not a condensed, difficult 'poetic' pair of images. They are in fact **fairly easy and 'natural'** precisely because the language facilitates these sorts of juxtapositions."

## 4. COORDINATION

### Noun Coordination (Simplest)

**No explicit conjunction required:**

```
李杜文章在
Li Du composition remain
= "The compositions of Li [Bai] and Du [Fu] remain"
```

**Fuller:**
> "Pairs of nouns present no problem at all. The only slight difference from English usage is that Classical Chinese does not require an explicit conjunction like 'and' or 'or'."

---

### Verb Coordination (Complex and Crucial)

**Two coordinate verbs typically imply a relationship**, but the **relationship type is not explicitly marked**.

#### Type 1: Sequential Actions (Action 1 → Action 2 = Result)

```
抽刀斷水水更流
pull knife break water water more flow
= "[I] pull a knife to cut the water, [but] the water flows all the more"

舉杯銷愁愁更愁
lift glass dispel sad sad more sad
= "[I] lift a glass to dispel sadness, [but] sadness becomes yet more sad"
```

**Relationship:**
- Action 1 is performed
- Action 2 is the (intended or actual) result

#### Type 2: Purpose Relations (Action 1 → Action 2 = Purpose)

```
煖湯濯我足
heat water wash my feet
= "[He] heated water [in order to] wash our feet"

剪紙招我魂
cut paper summon my soul
= "[He] cut paper [in order to] summon our souls"
```

**Relationship:**
- Action 1 is means
- Action 2 is purpose

---

### The Problem: Uncertain Relationships

**Fuller's most important syntactic insight:**

Sometimes the relationship between coordinated verbs **remains uncertain**, and this uncertainty becomes **the central interpretive problem** of the poem.

#### Du Fu's "Spring Vista" — The Defining Example

```
國破山河在
country break mountain river remain
= "The capital is broken: mountains and rivers remain"

城春草木深
wall spring grass tree deep
= "The city wall in spring: the grasses and trees grow thickly"
```

**Fuller's analysis:**
> "What is the relationship between the catastrophic An Lushan Rebellion and the constancy of the landscape that Du Fu observes? Is there solace to be found, or is the landscape entirely indifferent to the fate of empires? Is such indifference a model to emulate, or should Du Fu strive for constancy on a human scale?"

> "**The nature of Classical Chinese syntax suggests that surely the coordinate actions in the first line are related, but the relationship here remains uncertain.** Indeed, this **uncertainty in the relationship** between the human and the natural realm becomes the **central problem the poem explores**."

**Critical point:**
English translation MUST choose:
- Contrast: "The capital is broken, BUT the mountains remain"
- Cause: "BECAUSE the capital is broken, [only] the mountains remain"
- Temporal sequence: "The capital broke; THEN the mountains remained"
- Pure juxtaposition: "The capital is broken; the mountains remain" (neutral)

Chinese syntax **allows the uncertainty to remain**, making it an interpretive resource.

### Coverbs: Grammaticalized Coordinate Verbs

**Definition**: Verbs that have grammaticalized into function words expressing causal/conditional relationships

**Standard position:** Before main verb

| Coverb | Translation | Original Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 以 | with, by means of | to use |
| 從 | from | to follow |
| 自 | from | to start from |
| 與 | with | to accompany |
| 為 | for | to act for |
| 因 | relying on | to rely on |
| 由 | following | to follow route of |
| 緣 | following | to follow along |

**Standard word order:**
```
Coverb + [its object] + Main Verb
```

**Example:**
```
以茶待客
with tea serve guest
= "to serve guests with tea"
= "to use tea in order to serve guests"
```

**Reversal for rhetorical effect:**

**Fuller:**
> "However, because coverbs remain verbs in a coordinate relationship with the main verb, syntax allows the two to be reversed. When the main verb comes first, one needs to look for a rhetorical reason for the change in word order."

**Example:**
```
待客以茶
serve guest with tea
```

By fronting the main verb, the poet emphasizes the action over the means.

## Practical Analysis Steps

### Step 1: Identify Sentence Structure

Determine:
- Is this topic-comment or subject-predicate?
- Where is the topic? Where does the comment begin?
- Is the comment verbal or nominal?

### Step 2: Identify Object Types

For each verb, determine:
- Does it take a direct object?
- Is there an indirect object (recipient)?
- Is there a locative object (location/comparison/passive agent)?
- Is the locative marker (於) present or omitted?

### Step 3: Map Modification Relationships

Ask:
- Which words modify which?
- Are there alternative readings?
- Is the ambiguity intentional?

### Step 4: Analyze Coordination

For coordinated verbs/phrases:
- What is the relationship? (sequential, purpose, contrast, uncertain?)
- Is the uncertainty significant?
- How does English translation force a choice?

### Step 5: Check for Causative Constructions

Ask:
- Are stative verbs taking direct objects?
- Does this create animation or agency in the landscape?
- How does this affect meaning?

### Step 6: Explain Syntactic Effects

Be explicit about:
- How syntax creates meaning
- What English translation must add/choose
- Why syntactic ambiguity or special constructions matter
- How syntax enables poetic effects unavailable in English

## Common Pitfalls

### 1. Assuming Subject-Predicate Structure

**Error:** Looking for explicit subjects in every sentence
**Problem:** Misses topic-comment structure
**Solution:** Identify topics (what the sentence is about) vs. subjects (agents of verbs)

### 2. Missing Causative Constructions

**Error:** Reading all verbs as stative/descriptive
**Problem:** Loses animated quality of landscapes
**Solution:** Check if stative verbs take direct objects, creating causative reading

### 3. Over-Disambiguating Coordinate Verbs

**Error:** Choosing one relationship and ignoring uncertainty
**Problem:** Destroys intentional syntactic ambiguity
**Solution:** Acknowledge when relationship is uncertain and ask what that uncertainty contributes

### 4. Ignoring Locative Object Ambiguity

**Error:** Not noticing when 於 is omitted
**Problem:** Misses intentional ambiguity about directionality/agency
**Solution:** Check if locative relationship is marked or unmarked; analyze effect of ambiguity

### 5. Treating Nominal Sentences as Incomplete

**Error:** Assuming nominal comments need copula verbs
**Problem:** Imposes English grammar on Chinese
**Solution:** Recognize Topic + Noun Comment as complete sentence making identity statement

## Key Quotations from Fuller

> "Classical Chinese is a topic-prominent isolating language: that is, in Classical Chinese one need not include an explicit subject in a sentence—so pronouns are mostly dropped in poetry."

> "於 is a 'prosy' character usually avoided in poetry so that on occasion uncertainty can creep into a line. **This syntactic ambiguity becomes a resource that authors can exploit.**"

> "Weakening the 冷 from the active verb 'chills' to the stative verb 'cold,' however, destroys the animated quality of the landscape Wang Wei describes here. **This type of causative construction is important in poetry because it syntactically enforces a connection** between aspects of a scene that often are weakened in English translation."

> "The nature of Classical Chinese syntax suggests that surely the coordinate actions in the first line are related, but **the relationship here remains uncertain**. Indeed, this uncertainty in the relationship between the human and the natural realm becomes **the central problem the poem explores**."

> "Perhaps the key features to keep in mind—because they differ so much from English usage—are the **topic-comment structure** of sentences, the **importance of word order** in a language without grammatical affixes, and the corresponding **ease with which words shift from one word class to another**."

---

## Summary: Why Syntax Matters

Fuller establishes that syntactic analysis is **foundational** because:

1. **Topic-comment structure** creates rhetorical effects unavailable through English subject-predicate
2. **Locative objects** enable causative constructions that animate landscapes
3. **Omission of markers** creates intentional ambiguity about agency and directionality
4. **Coordination without explicit connectives** leaves relationships open for interpretation
5. **Syntactic uncertainty can be the poem's central interpretive problem**

Understanding Classical Chinese syntax reveals how **grammatical structure itself creates poetic meaning**.
