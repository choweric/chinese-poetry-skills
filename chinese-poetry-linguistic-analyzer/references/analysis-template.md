# Linguistic Analysis Template

## Fuller's Framework: Systematic Progression

Linguistic analysis should proceed **from foundation to interpretation**:

1. **Phonology** → Sound system, rhyme, tone
2. **Morphology** → Word class, ambiguity, compounds
3. **Syntax** → Sentence structure, relationships, constructions
4. **Poetic Effect** → How linguistic features create meaning
5. **Translation Loss** → What English cannot capture and why

---

## Complete Analysis Structure

### Step 1: Historical and Formal Context

**Establish phonological system:**
- What period? (Old Chinese, Middle Chinese, Early Mandarin)
- What form? (regulated verse, old style, lyric)
- What are the formal constraints?

**Questions:**
- Which sound system applies?
- If regulated verse, what are the tonal prosody rules?
- If old style, are there rhyme changes?

---

### Step 2: Phonological Analysis

#### 2.1 Identify Rhyme Scheme

**For regulated verse:**
- Mark rhyme words (lines 2, 4, 6, 8; optional line 1)
- Check rhyme group (all from same group?)
- Note tone category (level, rising, departing, entering)

**For old style:**
- Track where rhymes change
- Note any tone mixing
- Identify rhyme groups used

**Analysis:**
- What rhymes are created?
- Do rhymes unite semantic fields?
- Are there near-rhymes (tongyong)?

#### 2.2 Map Tonal Patterns (if Regulated Verse)

**Five-character pattern:**
```
Line 1: 仄仄平平仄
Line 2: 平平仄仄平 (rhyme)
```

**Seven-character pattern:**
```
Line 1: 仄仄 + 平平平仄仄
Line 2: 平平 + 仄仄仄平平 (rhyme)
```

**Analysis:**
- Map actual tones against expected pattern
- Identify violations (especially positions 2, 4, 6)
- Ask: Why this violation? What word is emphasized?

#### 2.3 Identify Sound Effects

Look for:
- Alliteration (repeated initial consonants)
- Assonance (repeated vowels/finals within lines)
- Onomatopoeia (sound-meaning correspondence)

**Analysis:**
- How does sound unite imagery?
- What aural textures are created?
- How does sound create emphasis?

---

### Step 3: Morphological Analysis

#### 3.1 Identify Word Classes

**For each key character, determine:**
- Noun or verb in this context?
- Could it be read differently?
- Is there morphological ambiguity?

**Look for:**
- Word class fluidity creating alternative readings
- Restricted modifiers (其, 不, 所, etc.) that clarify class
- Particles (之) that disambiguate

#### 3.2 Identify Poetic Compounds

**For two-character combinations:**
- What is the modification relationship?
- Verb modifying noun? ("bright moon")
- Noun modifying verb? ("mountain stillness")
- Noun + noun? ("human life")
- Verb + noun as predicate? ("moon bright")

**Analysis:**
- Does this create meaning unavailable in English?
- How does word class fluidity enable this?
- What would English translation require to express this?

#### 3.3 Check for Morphological Ambiguity

**Identify where lack of markers creates:**

**Number ambiguity:**
- Singular or plural nouns?
- Does ambiguity serve poetic purpose?

**Tense ambiguity:**
- Past, present, or future?
- Does timelessness matter?

**Aspect ambiguity:**
- Completed or ongoing action?
- Does uncertainty enrich meaning?

**Analysis:**
- What grammatical choices would English force?
- How does Chinese ambiguity create richer interpretation?
- Is the ambiguity intentional?

---

### Step 4: Syntactic Analysis

#### 4.1 Identify Sentence Structure

**For each clause:**
- Topic-comment or subject-predicate?
- Where is the topic?
- Is the comment verbal or nominal?

**Analysis:**
- How does topic-fronting create rhetorical emphasis?
- Are there layered structures (sentence as topic of larger sentence)?
- How does this differ from English subject-predicate?

#### 4.2 Identify Object Types

**For each verb:**
- Direct object? (entity acted upon)
- Indirect object? (recipient)
- Locative object? (location in space/time/relation)

**Critical questions:**
- Is locative marker (於) present or omitted?
- If omitted, does this create ambiguity?
- Is this a causative construction (stative verb + direct object)?

**Analysis:**
- How does locative object marking (or lack thereof) affect meaning?
- Do causative constructions animate the landscape?
- What agency relationships are created?

#### 4.3 Map Modification Relationships

**For each modification:**
- Which word modifies which?
- Are there alternative readings?
- Is ambiguity created by lack of 之?

**Analysis:**
- How does modification create poetic juxtaposition?
- What relationships between perceptions are explored?
- Could this be read as complete sentence instead?

#### 4.4 Analyze Coordination

**For coordinate verbs/phrases:**
- What relationship is implied?
  - Sequential (action 1 → action 2 as result)?
  - Purpose (action 1 → action 2 as goal)?
  - Contrast?
  - Uncertain?

**Analysis:**
- Is the relationship explicitly marked or ambiguous?
- If uncertain, is this the poem's central problem?
- What would English translation have to choose?
- Are there coverbs? Are they in standard or reversed position?

---

### Step 5: Poetic Effect Analysis

#### 5.1 Compression and Poetic Economy

**How linguistic features enable compression:**
- Topic-comment allows subject omission
- Word class fluidity creates compounds
- Lack of grammatical markers reduces characters

**Analysis:**
- How many characters express the core meaning?
- How many English words are needed?
- What is gained by compression?

#### 5.2 Intentional Ambiguity as Resource

**Where does linguistic structure create multiple readings?**
- Morphological ambiguity (number, tense, word class)
- Syntactic ambiguity (locative relationships, coordination)
- Modification ambiguity (which modifies which)

**Analysis:**
- Are multiple readings equally valid?
- What does the ambiguity contribute?
- Does it enrich or complicate interpretation?

#### 5.3 Animated Landscapes and Agency

**How do syntactic structures create animation?**
- Causative constructions (landscapes as agents)
- Locative ambiguity (who acts on whom)
- Topic-fronting (non-human topics)

**Analysis:**
- How are human and natural realms related?
- What agency is attributed to landscape?
- How does syntax enforce connections?

#### 5.4 Perceptual Juxtaposition

**How do modification rules explore perception?**
- Unexpected adjective-noun combinations
- Verbs modifying nouns to transfer qualities
- Nouns modifying verbs to create abstractions

**Analysis:**
- What "carving up of perception" occurs?
- How do perceptions inform one another?
- What conceptual relationships are created?

---

### Step 6: Translation Loss Analysis

#### 6.1 What English Must Add

**Grammatical requirements English imposes:**
- Explicit subjects/pronouns
- Number markers (singular/plural)
- Tense markers (past/present/future)
- Aspect markers (progressive, perfect)
- Copula verbs in nominal sentences
- Prepositions for relationships
- Conjunctions for coordination

**Analysis:**
- Which additions change meaning?
- What interpretive choices are forced?

#### 6.2 What English Cannot Capture

**Sound effects:**
- Specific rhymes
- Tonal patterns
- Alliteration/assonance

**Morphological effects:**
- Word class fluidity
- Poetic compounds
- Systematic ambiguity

**Syntactic effects:**
- Topic-comment rhetorical focus
- Causative constructions
- Coordination without connectives
- Locative ambiguity

**Analysis:**
- What is fundamentally untranslatable?
- How does translation weaken the poem?
- What alternate strategies could translation use?

#### 6.3 Why This Matters for Interpretation

**Fuller's central argument:**
> "Whatever translation one offers compels one to take a position about the relationship that Du Fu can leave in suspension because of the nature of the Classical Chinese language."

**Critical questions:**
- How do linguistic features create the poem's core meanings?
- What interpretations are enabled by Chinese structure that English destroys?
- How is "form an extension of content"?

## Example Analysis Outline

### Poem: Du Fu, "Spring Vista" (春望)

#### Step 1: Context
- Period: High Tang (Middle Chinese phonology)
- Form: Five-character regulated verse (lüshi)
- Historical context: An Lushan Rebellion (755–763)

#### Step 2: Phonology
**Rhyme:**
- Line 1: 在 [tsən, departing tone] — no rhyme
- Line 2: 深 [sim, level tone] — RHYME
- Line 3: 淚 — no rhyme
- Line 4: 心 [sim, level tone] — RHYME (same as line 2)

Analysis: Perfect rhyme on lines 2 and 4 (-im finals, level tone). Sonically unites "deep vegetation" (深) with "startled heart" (心).

**Tonal pattern violations:**
[Would map actual tones and identify any significant violations]

#### Step 3: Morphology

**Line 1: 國破山河在**
- 破 (break): verb (stative or causative?)
- 在 (remain): verb
- Word class clear; minimal morphological ambiguity

**Line 3: 感時花濺淚**
- 感 (moved): verb, but who is moved?
- 濺 (splash): verb, but who splashes?
- 淚 (tears): noun, but whose tears?

Analysis: Lack of number/person markers creates ambiguity:
1. Du Fu is moved → splashes tears
2. Flowers are moved → splash (dew as) tears
3. Both readings coexist

#### Step 4: Syntax

**Line 1: Topic-comment with coordination**
```
國破 || 山河在
topic: 國 (capital)
comment: 破 (broken) — complete sentence

Larger structure:
topic: "the capital is broken"
comment: "mountains and rivers remain"
```

**Coordination problem:**
What is relationship between "broken" and "remain"?
- Contrast? Cause? Temporal sequence? Uncertain?

Fuller: "This **uncertainty in the relationship** between the human and the natural realm becomes the **central problem the poem explores**."

**Line 3: Locative ambiguity**
```
感時花濺淚
```

Possible readings:
1. 感時 (moved by times) modifies implicit subject "I" → [I] splash tears on flowers
2. 感時 modifies 花 (flowers) → flowers splash [their dew as] tears
3. Both

No locative marker present; relationship ambiguous.

#### Step 5: Poetic Effect

**Compression:**
- 20 characters express complex meditation on war, nature, human emotion
- English requires 50+ words

**Intentional ambiguity:**
- Morphological: whose tears? whose emotion?
- Syntactic: what relationship between human and natural?
- Creates multiple valid interpretations

**Animated landscape:**
- Causative reading possible: "country breaks [the] mountains [but] rivers remain"
- Landscape has constancy human realm lacks

#### Step 6: Translation Loss

**What English must add:**
- Explicit subject (I vs. flowers)
- Number (mountain vs. mountains)
- Tense (is broken vs. has been broken)
- Conjunction (but, because, when)

**What English loses:**
- Rhyme between "deep" and "heart"
- Tonal pattern effects
- Ambiguity about agency and emotion
- Open relationship between human and natural

**Why it matters:**
English translation **forces interpretive choices** that Chinese leaves suspended. The uncertainty IS the meaning.

## Common Patterns in Tang Poetry

### Pattern 1: Causative Landscapes

Stative verbs take direct objects, creating animated natural world:
- "Sun chills the pines"
- "Hamlet sends up smoke"
- "Bamboo coolness invades bedroom"

**Effect**: Syntactically enforces connections between human and natural

### Pattern 2: Locative Ambiguity

Omission of 於 creates uncertainty about directionality:
- "Tiles sing snow" (tiles make snow sing? or snow makes tiles sing?)
- "Duck illuminates lamp" (duck lights lamp? or lamp lights duck?)

**Effect**: Dynamic interplay between scene elements

### Pattern 3: Coordinate Verb Uncertainty

Relationship between actions left open:
- "Capital broken; mountains remain" (contrast? cause? sequence? uncertain?)
- Du Fu's central technique

**Effect**: Uncertainty becomes the poem's interpretive problem

### Pattern 4: Morphological Ambiguity

Who acts? Singular or plural? Past or present?
- "Moved by times, flowers splash tears"
- Multiple valid readings coexist

**Effect**: Enriches interpretation; forces English to choose

## Key Principle from Fuller

> "Perhaps the key features to keep in mind—because they differ so much from English usage—are the **topic-comment structure** of sentences, the **importance of word order** in a language without grammatical affixes, and the corresponding **ease with which words shift from one word class to another**."

Linguistic analysis is **foundational, not ornamental**. Understanding how the language works reveals how poetic meaning is created.
